Gym Industry Economics

Membership-driven revenue, retention economics, and why MRR is the core metric for gym operators and buyers.

  • Membership-driven economics

    Gym businesses typically generate recurring monthly revenue through memberships while supplementing income through personal training, group classes, and retail sales.

  • Retention beats acquisition

    Member retention often has a greater impact on profitability than new member acquisition. A 5% improvement in monthly retention can materially change annual profit.

  • MRR is the core metric

    Monthly recurring revenue (MRR) from memberships is the foundation of gym valuation. Operators who track MRR, churn, and revenue per member make better pricing and staffing decisions.

  • Valuation follows recurring quality

    Businesses with strong recurring revenue, low churn, and diversified income streams generally achieve higher valuations than single-revenue-model gyms.

Frequently Asked Questions

How profitable is a gym?

Healthy independent gyms typically achieve 12–22% net profit margin, with a median around 18%. Operators with strong personal training penetration, low churn, and efficient staffing can reach 20%+. High rent markets compress margins below 12%.

What is average gym revenue?

The median independent gym generates about $1.2M in annual revenue. The interquartile range spans $650K (25th percentile) to $1.8M (75th percentile), with top-performing multi-location operators exceeding $2.5M.

How much do gym owners make?

Gym owners typically earn $85K–$160K in total compensation, with a median around $120K for owner-operators of established single-location gyms. Multi-location operators with strong MRR and training revenue can exceed $250K.

What is a gym business worth?

Gym businesses typically sell at 2.8×–4.2× SDE, with a median near 3.5×. A gym with $1.2M revenue and $280K SDE might value between $780K and $1.2M. Low churn and high recurring revenue support premium multiples.

How important is member retention?

Member retention is critical — it often matters more than new sales. Improving monthly churn from 5% to 4% can add six figures in annual revenue without additional marketing spend. Buyers heavily weight retention when valuing gym businesses.

What is a good churn rate for a gym?

Industry-wide annual retention is 66.4% per the HFA 2025 Benchmarking Report — roughly one in three members cancels each year. That implies roughly 3–4% monthly churn in a steady-state model. Budget gyms often run 5–8% monthly; boutique and community-driven formats may achieve 2–4%.

How many members does a gym need to be profitable?

Most independent gyms break even between 350–500 active members depending on rent, payroll, and equipment debt. At HFA median dues (~$38/mo) plus training revenue, 800–1,200+ active members typically supports healthy margins for a single-location operator.

Operator benchmark sources

  • Health & Fitness Association (HFA) — Benchmarking Report — 2025 Fitness Industry Benchmarking Report: 66.4% U.S. member retention, monthly dues, and operator financial ratios (175-company sample, 114 U.S. respondents).
  • Health & Fitness Association (HFA) — Industry revenue data — Published U.S. club benchmarks including ~$517 average annual revenue per member and $69 mean / $38 median monthly dues (2024).
  • Business-for-sale listings (BizBuySell, fitness brokers) — Gym and fitness center listings with disclosed revenue, SDE, and member counts (2023–2026) for owner-level economics.
  • IRS Statistics of Income — Schedule C (NAICS 713940) — Sole-proprietorship revenue and expense ratios for fitness and recreational sports centers.

Industry & macro data sources

  • IBISWorld — Gym, Health & Fitness Clubs (NAICS 713940) — Industry revenue, establishment counts, and margin ranges for U.S. fitness clubs.
  • Published fitness marketing CAC studies — Third-party CAC benchmarks for budget through mid-range clubs ($63–$249+ blended); used as ranges, not single-point estimates.

Calculation formulas

  • Revenue per member

    Annual Member Revenue ÷ 12

    HFA industry estimate: ~$517/member/year ≈ $43/mo. Independent gyms with personal training often run $43–$65/mo.

  • Member lifetime value (LTV)

    Revenue per Member per Month × Average Membership Lifespan (months)

    Typical range $500–$900 for mid-market independents. Lifespan varies widely by onboarding, format, and churn.

  • Customer acquisition cost (CAC)

    (Sales + Marketing Spend) ÷ New Paying Members

    Published benchmarks span $63–$249+ for budget-to-mid-range clubs; referral channels typically lowest.

  • Monthly recurring revenue (MRR)

    Annual Revenue × Membership Revenue Share ÷ 12

    Estimated ~$62K/mo for a ~$1.2M gym with ~62% membership revenue — a modeled figure, not an HFA direct report.

  • Implied monthly churn

    1 − (Annual Retention Rate)^(1/12)

    66.4% annual retention (HFA) implies ~3.4% monthly churn in a steady-state model; actual clubs report 3–8% depending on format.

  • SDE valuation

    SDE × Industry Multiple (median 3.5×)

    SDE = Net Profit + Owner Compensation + add-backs. Range 2.8×–4.2× SDE from business-for-sale comps.

Limitations

  • Member economics (dues, retention, revenue/member) primarily reflect HFA U.S. club data — a mix of independents, franchises, and multi-club operators.
  • HFA retention (66.4%) is annual; monthly churn varies by onboarding quality, format, and price tier.
  • CAC and LTV are presented as ranges because marketing channel mix and local competition create wide variance.
  • MRR and owner-level revenue/margin figures are composite estimates — not substitutes for your own books, CPA review, or formal valuation.
